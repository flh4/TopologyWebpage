Set Theory

Definition: Let A and B be two sets. Then:

The union of sets A and B is defined by:

$A \cup B = \{ x:x \in A \: or \: x \in B \}.$

The intersection of sets A and B is defined by:

$A \cap B = \{ x:x \in A \: and \: x \in B \}.$

Theorem: The following statements are equivalent for any two sets A and B.

1. $A \subset B$
2. $A \cup B = B$
3. $A \cap B = A$

Proof: Suppose $A \subset B$ and consider $A \cup B.$ Since $A \subset B$ then $A \cup B \subset B \cup B = B.$ So $A \cup B \subset B.$ But $B \subset A \cup B.$ Hence $A \cup B = B.$

Theorem: For any sets A, B, and C:

1.  $A \cup (B \cap C) = (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C)$

Proof: Consider   $A \cup (B \cap C) = \{ x:x \in A \: or \: x \in B \cap C \}.$
                               $= \{x:x \in A \: or \: x \in B \: and \: x \in C\}$
                               $= \{x:x \in A \: or \: x \in B \: and \: x \in A \: or \: x \in C\}$
                               $= \{x:x \in A \cup B \: and \: x \in A \cup C\}$
                               $= (A \cup B) \cap (A \cup C).$

2.  $A \cap (B \cup C) = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap C)$

Definition: Let A and B be subsets of a universal set X.

The complement of A with respect to X is defined by:

$X \backslash A = \{x:x \in X \: and \: x \notin A \}.$
